# Why We Built Klystora in 27 Languages

Word games are fundamentally about **language**. And language is fundamentally about **culture**. This is why Klystora exists in 27 languages — not as translations, but as native experiences.

## The Problem with "Translated" Word Games

Most "multilingual" word games are:
1. Built in English
2. Translated by algorithm
3. Deployed globally

This doesn't work because:

### Word Frequency Differs

The letter E is common in English. In Spanish, it's also common — but Ñ is unique. In German, ÄÖÜ change everything. A game balanced for English is unbalanced for other languages.

### Cultural References Don't Translate

A clue like "NYC subway line" means nothing to someone in Mexico City. "Famous Spanish painter" is obvious to a Spaniard but not to a Korean player.

### Dictionary Quality Varies

English has SOWPODS and TWL. Spanish has DRAE. German has Duden. Each has different rules for what counts as a valid word. A translated game uses the wrong dictionary.

## Our Approach: Native-First

For each of our 27 languages, we:

1. **Curate a native dictionary** — not translated from English
2. **Calibrate difficulty per language** — letter frequency, word length, common patterns
3. **Write clues culturally** — references that make sense to native speakers
4. **Test with native speakers** — not Google Translate

## The Technical Challenge

Supporting 27 languages means:
- 27 dictionaries (curated, not machine-translated)
- 27 sets of game slugs (e.g., `/es/palabra-diaria`, `/de/taegliches-wort`)
- 27 SEO keyword strategies
- 27 content calendars

It's more work. But it's the only way to do it right.
